For decades, the Democratic Republic of the Congo has been the site of nearly unbroken conflict. It is a crisis that, by some estimates, has become the deadliest since World War II, with many millions killed.

Capturing the weight of this in words is nearly impossible. Yet one group of artists and activists has spent years trying: Congolese slam poets, whose verses have become a tool of resistance and release for a new generation of young people in the country.

To help share their perspectives more widely, The New Humanitarian has partnered with five slammers from eastern Congo — the region hardest hit by conflict — who have created new poems confronting the realities of war and honoring the strength of their communities.

Their words have never been more urgent.

For the past three years, violence has escalated sharply in the east as a rebel group known as the March 23 Movement or M23 has launched one of the most destabilising insurgencies in years. 

Entire cities have fallen to the rebels, forcing millions of people to flee their homes, including artists and slammers. It is one of the worst crises in the world in terms of humanitarian impact, yet global media attention has been desperately limited.

Still, while many look away, the poets continue to speak out. Some of them are still living in eastern Congo – in the rebel-occupied cities of Goma and Bukavu. Others have gone into exile for their safety.

All share a common purpose: to speak the truth of what is happening – and what it feels like to experience war and displacement.

A performance-driven form of spoken word with bite, slam poetry began to take root in Congo a decade ago. Emerging from a vibrant local art and activist scene, it gave people a powerful way to speak out against injustice.

Though the current conflict has upended their work, slam collectives in Goma and Bukavu have spent years not only writing and performing, but taking their art into the public sphere, visiting schools and raising awareness around social and political issues.

The poems you are about to watch tell deeply personal stories — of exile, of the authors witnessing atrocities, of lives disrupted by war. 

Some take a longer view, confronting the legacy of colonial exploitation and the ongoing plunder of Congo’s vast mineral wealth — cobalt, coltan, copper — by international companies and neighbouring states.

All portray the violence that Congolese are forced to endure, while also celebrating the resilience of its people. “For over a century we've been dying,” says Patricia Kamoso, a poet in Bukavu, “but we're not dead. It's our greatest victory: We're not dead”.

Goma, my living dead city

By Soleil, in Goma

 

In this piece, Soleil, who is using a pseudonym, speaks about pain and resilience in Goma, the biggest city in eastern Congo, while denouncing the violence inflicted on innocent people. Through a poignant message of hope, she shows that art is a healing tool, giving a voice to those who suffer in silence and reminding us of the importance of remembering struggles for justice and dignity.

Though Soleil has chosen to be anonymous, she is a well-known member of the slam community in Goma having performed at major festivals inside and outside Congo. Her commitment to the cause of women and her ability to articulate daily struggles through her poems has sparked profound reflection among audiences.

|

She said: “I wrote this text to put down on paper all the pain I can't bear and all the trauma we would like to erase from our minds. I wrote it to restore hope, to cry out on behalf of my entire community that suffers in silence. I wrote it so that even those who no longer believe in change would lean toward my belief in victory after a long struggle. Even as an artist, there is a particular way of looking at us, at other people. We can feel that they rely heavily on us, to heal their wounds through our words and to be their voices to claim their rights. That's why I wrote this text.”

Who is Hope?

By Jenny Paria, in Goma

 

In this work, Nzego Grâce Isaac, known by his stage name Jenny Paria, celebrates resilience in the face of oppression. The poem emphasises that, even in uncertainty, everyone carries hope and light within them. Despite the constraints of their environment, the artists of Goma continue to fight for their memory and dignity, demonstrating that hope is a form of resistance in the face of indifference.

A rapper, poet, and singer, Jenny Paria is founding member of the Goma Slam Session collective. He was a finalist for the 2024 RFI Discovery Award, the winner of the 2024 Afro Pépite Award, a semi-finalist at the 2022 World Slam Championship in Belgium, and a national slam champion in DRC.

He has participated in various social initiatives such as "Slam at School" and slam therapy. He is also involved in humanitarian work, having worked until 2024 with the organization AGIR RDC as a community facilitator in displacement camps.

|

He said: “Between the inability to leave and the difficulty of staying. Between the desire to live and the obligation to adapt. Faced with a vague present and an uncertain future. I'm not here to condemn, accuse, point fingers, or complain. I just want to remind us that we've always been resilient, and today more than ever; we must hope.”

We are not dead

By Patricia Kamoso, in Bukavu

 

In Patricia Kamoso's work, survival emerges as an essential victory. She evokes pain and the fight against violence while honoring the memory of those who have died. Her message of hope and resilience inspires us to fight for peace and dignity, essential for future generations.

Born in Bukavu in 2001, Patricia discovered slam poetry in 2016 and took her first steps on stage in 2018.  She is currently coordinator of BUKAVU SLAM SESSION and a slam-poetry trainer. After several training courses in directing and acting, she embarked on a career in theater and performed for the first time in a play entitled "Cri du cœur," a play by director Magloire Bolunda from Kisangani. She has launched a women's slam program collective and has toured her own solo slam show entitled "Who Are You?" across the Great Lakes region.  She has participated in several festivals in the region Her artistic approach is based on human rights, peace and resilience.

|

She said: “This text is a tribute to the dead and a celebration of life. I wrote it while traveling on a bus, where passengers were talking about what the war had taken from them, especially the people they had lost. This text embodies all the emotions I felt at that moment. During this time of war, this text is a way for me to honor those we have lost, but at the same time, to be grateful to still be alive. Because right now, we're all trying to survive.”

My voice

By DePaul Bakulu Remy, in Tanzania

 

This poem from Depaul Bakulu, an artist, slam poet, and activist from Goma who is currently exiled in Tanzania, delves into the pain caused by war, transforming his suffering into a voice of resistance. The slam calls for memory and justice, revealing that creativity can shine even in darkness.

DePaul has been involved in social, cultural, and environmental struggles for several years. He is the co-founder of the Goma Slam Session collective and an active member of the Lucha DRC and Extinction Rebellion Goma movements. In April 2025, he received the prestigious Global Advocacy Award from Harvard Law School, which recognises his artistic and activist commitment to human rights and vulnerable communities. Exiled in Arusha due to his commitment to human rights, he uses poetry as a tool of resistance, remembrance, and healing.

|

He said: “This text bears witness to the suffering caused by the war in eastern DRC. It evokes shattered projects, stolen dreams, loved ones buried without tribute, and the silence surrounding the victims. It denounces impunity, political negotiations that ignore the victims, and the pain of survivors as a result of capitalism. It also speaks of the power of poetry, which heals inner wounds and transforms pain into resistance against hatred and violence. This text calls for justice, memory, and dignity to heal the scars of a forgotten people.”

To believe it

By Osée Elektra, in Tanzania 

 

In the poem by Osée Elektra, writing becomes an act of resistance. Poetry, amidst chaos, restores hope and raises fundamental questions about the future. This slam calls for a world where art is a right and emphasises the need to continue writing, as an act of survival in the fight against injustice. The video was filmed anonymously while Osée was still in Goma, but now exiled in Tanzania, he is happy for us to use his name.

Osée is a slam poet, rapper, and activist whose work has a unique style called "Slam Elektrik”, blending local folklore with contemporary sounds. Driven by a committed pen, he uses art to raise awareness and revive hope. Author of several singles and an EP entitled "Cocktail," he performs on numerous regional and international stages. Nicknamed "Soldier," he embodies an artistic voice of resistance and unity. He is also the artistic director of JUA-Asbl, an organisation that uses arts and culture to promote social engagement in the Great Lakes region.

|

He said: "To believe it" is a slam of resistance born from the chaos currently engulfing the Kivu region, where writing becomes an act of survival and freedom. The text uses poetry to heal wounds, question the future, and give voice to those we want to silence. It dreams of a world where art is no longer a crime, but a bridge to peace. It is a cry of hope launched through metaphors. To believe in it, we write.”
